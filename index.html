Feeding & Digestion

Jellyfish capture prey using tentacles covered with stinging cells called nematocysts. Once prey is immobilized, it is moved to the mouth and digested in a central cavity called the gastrovascular cavity, where enzymes break it down.

  • Encyclopaedia Britannica: Digestion

Respiration

Jellyfish do not have lungs or gills. Oxygen and carbon dioxide move in and out of their bodies by diffusion through thin tissues directly from the surrounding water.

Learn more

  • NOAA: How Jellyfish Breathe
  • ScienceDirect: Diffusion

Circulation

Jellyfish do not have a heart or blood vessels. Nutrients are distributed throughout the body by the movement of fluids inside the gastrovascular cavity.

Response

Jellyfish have a simple nerve net instead of a brain. This allows them to sense light, touch, and chemicals in the water and respond by moving or stinging.

Movement & Support

Jellyfish move by rhythmically contracting and relaxing their bell, pushing water behind them. Their body is supported by water pressure and a jelly-like substance called mesoglea.

Reproduction

Jellyfish can reproduce both sexually and asexually. Adult jellyfish release eggs and sperm into the water, while polyps can reproduce asexually by budding.

Homeostasis

Jellyfish maintain balance by living in stable aquatic environments. Their bodies naturally match the temperature and salt concentration of the surrounding seawater.

Life Cycle of a Jellyfish

Jellyfish have a fascinating life cycle with several stages. It begins as a fertilized egg that develops into a free-swimming larva called a planula. The planula attaches to a surface and becomes a polyp. The polyp then reproduces asexually, releasing young jellyfish called ephyrae. These grow into adult jellyfish, also called medusae.

Moon Jellyfish

Moon jellyfish are transparent and gentle, commonly found in coastal waters around the world.

Box Jellyfish

Box jellyfish have cube-shaped bells and complex eyes, allowing them to actively hunt prey.

Lion's Mane Jellyfish

The lion's mane jellyfish is one of the largest jellyfish species in the world.

Immortal Jellyfish

This species can revert back to its juvenile stage, making it biologically "immortal."

Flower Hat Jellyfish

A beautifully colored jellyfish species native to the waters around Japan.

Cannonball Jellyfish

Round-bodied jellyfish commonly found in warm coastal waters.

Sea Nettle

Sea nettles are known for their long tentacles and painful sting.

Crystal Jellyfish

This transparent jellyfish is famous for its natural bioluminescence.

Atolla Jellyfish

A deep-sea jellyfish with a striking red color.

Upside-Down Jellyfish

Lives upside down on the ocean floor, hosting algae for energy.

Blue Button Jellyfish

A small, blue species often found near beaches.

Comb Jelly

Comb jellies move using rows of tiny cilia instead of tentacles.
